Internal Proposal — For Editor in Chief Review
 

A Smarter Way
to Publish

 

How Swifts Coalition can go from Discord conversations to published articles — without the chaos.

 
   
   
The Big Picture
   

What We're Building

   

Right now, getting an article from a writer to the website involves a lot of back and forth. Someone writes something, sends it over, someone else formats it, uploads it, finds the images, assigns the author, hits publish. It works, but it's slow and things fall through the cracks.

   

What we're proposing is a system that lives inside our Discord server and does most of the heavy lifting automatically. A writer fills out a simple form right inside Discord. A bot reads it, checks that everything is in order, and sends it to the website as a draft. Staff review it, and when they're happy, they hit publish. That's it.

   
     

"Writers write. Editors edit. The bot handles everything in between."

   
   

No one needs to touch the WordPress dashboard unless they want to. No more copy-pasting articles. No more hunting for images. No more forgetting to set the author.

 
   
   
How Writers Submit
   

The Template

   

Every article starts with a template. Think of it like a form, but it lives in Discord and you just fill it out in plain text. A writer pastes it into the submissions channel, fills in their details, and posts it along with their images.

   

Here's what a filled-out template looks like:

   
TITLE: City Council Votes to Cut Parks Budget
AUTHOR: Jamie Rivera
CATEGORY: Local Government
TAGS: parks, budget, city council
FEATURED_IMAGE: council-chambers.jpg

---

The vote came just after midnight on Tuesday, with five council members in favor and two opposed. Residents who had packed the chambers earlier in the evening had mostly gone home by then.

[IMAGE: protest-outside.jpg | Residents protest outside City Hall | alt: A crowd of people holding handmade signs outside City Hall at night]

The proposed cuts would affect twelve parks across the district, eliminating weekend maintenance crews and closing two recreation centers by the end of the fiscal year.

[IMAGE: parks-map.jpg | Map showing affected parks | alt: A city map with twelve parks highlighted in red]

Council member Diaz, one of the two dissenting votes, said the decision sets a troubling precedent for how the city treats public green space.    
   

The writer attaches their image files to the same Discord message. They name the files themselves before uploading, so they know exactly what to call them in the template. Clean names only — the bot will fix any spaces or strange characters automatically and show the writer the corrected names.

 
   
   
Who Does What
   

Three Tiers, Clear Lines

   

The system has three types of people, each with a specific role. Nobody can do something they're not supposed to.

   
     
       

Contributors

       
             
  • Submit articles using the template
    •          
  • Attach images to their submission
    •          
  • Resubmit if something gets flagged
    •          
  • Cannot publish or edit others' work
    •          
  • Cannot pull drafts from the website
    •        
     
     
       

Editors & Staff

       
             
  • Review drafts before they go live
    •          
  • Pull any article back for edits
    •          
  • Hand edited templates back to writers
    •          
  • Override author assignments if needed
    •          
  • Approve and publish with one click
    •        
     
   
   

Writers write. Editors decide what's ready. Nobody steps on anyone else's toes because the system simply won't let them.

 
   
   
Author Pages
   

Real Author Pages. No Accounts Required.

   

Every article will show a proper author — with their name, a bio page, and a list of everything they've written. But here's the thing: authors don't need to sign up for a WordPress account. They don't need a password. They don't need to touch the website at all.

   

The first time a writer's name appears in a template, the system creates their author profile automatically. A notification goes to staff saying a new author was created so someone can fill out their bio and photo when there's time. After that, every article they submit gets linked to their profile automatically.

   

If a name is misspelled in a template, or a writer forgets to add their name, a staff member can override it before the article goes live. The writer never has to know anything changed.

 
   
   
Images
   

Images That Go Exactly Where You Put Them

   

Writers decide where images appear in the article by placing a simple tag in the text. The bot reads those tags, takes the image files that were uploaded with the submission, and places them exactly where indicated on the website — with captions and alt text intact.

   

If a template references an image that wasn't uploaded, the whole submission gets flagged and nothing goes to the website. The bot tells the writer exactly which image is missing. No broken pages, no placeholders, no guessing.

   

If a writer forgets to declare a featured image, the bot waits two minutes for a response, then defaults to the first image uploaded. Staff always see a note in the review channel when this happens, so nothing slips through without their knowing.

 
   
   
Making Changes
   

Editing Is Simple. On Purpose.

   

Contributors cannot edit drafts on their own. If something needs to change — whether it's a typo, a missing image, or a full rewrite — the process goes through staff.

   

A staff member pastes the article's link into Discord. The bot fetches the current version of that article and hands back a fully filled-out template, ready to copy. The staff member passes that to the writer. The writer makes their changes and submits it like a brand new article. The bot knows it's an edit because the link is embedded in the template itself, and it updates the existing draft on the website rather than creating a new one.

   

Staff then review the updated draft before anything changes on the live site.
